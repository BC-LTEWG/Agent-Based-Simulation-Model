F is derived from C due to the absence of a silicon or electronic substrate commodity series in the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics Producer Price Index (PPI) Commodity Data.
F represents a technologically refined nonmetallic mineral input—specifically high-purity silicon—whose price differs from raw nonmetallic minerals due to additional socially necessary labor, capital intensity, purification, and fabrication processes rather than a distinct natural material base.

In normalized prices:
F = (1.8 / 0.3) × C

G is derived from A due to the absence of a unified “rare metals” or advanced alloy commodity series in the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics PPI Commodity Data.
G represents a scarce or technologically complex metallic input whose higher price reflects scarcity, alloying complexity, and increased labor and capital requirements relative to common metals, while remaining materially grounded in the same metallic production base as iron and steel.

In normalized prices:
G = 2.5 × A

Pricing methodology

 - I replaced the original arbitrary ratios of the eight base product types with empirically grounded ratios derived from U.S. 
 Producer Price Index (PPI) commodity series published by the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics. This yields a 
 dynamic system of relative prices calibrated to observed U.S. production prices, expressed in terms of a common numéraire.

 - Since I designated common metal (iron/steel) as the numéraire, all other prices are therefore expressed as ratios 
 relative to the price of common industrial metal, reflecting relative production costs under U.S. conditions.

 - To convert these unitless relative prices into absolute money prices, I intend to scale the entire price vector using a 
 benchmark U.S. steel price. Specifically, I plan to use the 2024 benchmark hot-rolled coil (HRC) steel price reported by 
 the CME Group, which provides a representative market price for common industrial metal. This fixes the monetary 
 scale of the price system while preserving relative prices.

 - Because the United States is the reference country in the World Bank’s PPP framework, international dollars are 
 numerically identical to U.S. dollars (PPP conversion factor = 1). As a result, no currency conversion is required, 
 and all prices are expressed consistently in 2024 USD.
